Prompt engineering

Have you ever wondered why the LLM you are using, say ChatGPT or Claude, provides a lot of additional information that you are not necessarily looking for and why certain other apps that use the same underlying LLM/GPT models provide more targeted responses? One way we obtain more specific answers from our models is through "prompt engineering".

In this post I talk about prompt engineering from a practical perspective and share some insights based upon my experience. I describe some of the most commonly used approaches.

Prompt engineering involves a deep understanding of the model's capabilities, idiosyncrasies, and limitations, making it a blend of both science and art. Implementing apps that use prompt engineering can be technically challenging, so it requires coding and deployment skills too.

Using a GPT for volatility prediction in risk management

AI now has many more permutations of input/output, i.e., text-to-image (OpenAI's DALL·E), and text-to-video (Google's Imagen). I trained a small text-to-text GPT (Karpathy's NanoGPT) on high-frequency OHLC data and it performed well at volatility prediction. This is a very interesting result indeed that suggests LLMs can be trained and used for risk management.

NanoGPT is a smaller and more lightweight version of the larger GPT models, like ChatGPT and GPT-4. It uses a simple bigram language model and a transformer architecture with multi-head self-attention. I convert numbers to text and train the text-to-text nanoGPT to understand the patterns in the data well enough to generate text in the form of OHLC stock price data that can be switched back to numbers and used for complex numeric computations.
