AI at Scale: Projects & Insights

Welcome to my personal space where I build and scale AI using Deep Learning, Language Models, and GPTs. Here, I share my work and explore AI topics that I find interesting.

This site is a composite of several AI applications and a variety of HTML-based posts. Each of my AI applications is architected as a microservice, functioning independently while also forming part of an integrated ecosystem.

The front end uses Flask, a lightweight WSGI web application framework, along with the combination of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript to provide intuitive and responsive UI. Flask's ease of use and flexibility is ideal for my purposes.

My platform is hosted on Google Cloud, utilizing Nginx as a web server and proxy, and orchestrated through Kubernetes (K8s) for managing the microservices, ensuring smooth deployment, scaling, and networking.

The source code that drives my platform resides across multiple repositories on my GitHub. This multi-repo strategy facilitates modular development and efficient version control. If you're interested in delving deeper into the mechanics behind the services you see here, you're welcome to explore the GitHub repositories to understand my codebase further.

Using a GPT for volatility prediction in risk management

AI now has many more permutations of input/output, i.e., text-to-image (OpenAI's DALL·E), and text-to-video (Google's Imagen). I trained a small text-to-text GPT (Karpathy's NanoGPT) on high-frequency OHLC data and it performed well at volatility prediction. This is a very interesting result indeed that suggests LLMs can be trained and used for risk management.

NanoGPT is a smaller and more lightweight version of the larger GPT models, like ChatGPT and GPT-4. It uses a simple bigram language model and a transformer architecture with multi-head self-attention. I convert numbers to text and train the text-to-text nanoGPT to understand the patterns in the data well enough to generate text in the form of OHLC stock price data that can be switched back to numbers and used for complex numeric computations.

Responsible AI

I built the AI chatbot opposite, which employs OpenAI's GPT-4 language model to generate company-specific analyses. Select a report type (business, investigation, finance), input a valid company name, and the AI generates a tailored report. Notice the application of rigorous input validation and specific response boundaries, a cornerstone of responsible AI.

Key principles of responsible AI implementation include:

  • Misuse Prevention: Input validation blocks harmful or inappropriate AI outputs and potential misuse.
  • User Safety: Techniques like input validation safeguard sensitive personal information, aligning with privacy laws.
  • Output Quality: Prompt engineering steers the AI to generate detailed, accurate responses, in this case specific types of analysis.
  • Ethical Guidelines Adherence: Input validation and prompt engineering ensure respect for societal norms and ethical guidelines.
  • Biases Mitigation: Careful input validation and prompt engineering can control amplification of existing biases.

This AI chatbot therefore illustrates how input validation and prompt engineering are crucial in maintaining responsible, safe, and high-quality AI outputs.

Prompt engineering

Have you ever wondered why the LLM you are using, say ChatGPT or Claude, provides a lot of additional information that you are not necessarily looking for and why certain other apps that use the same underlying LLM/GPT models provide more targeted responses? One way we obtain more specific answers from our models is through "prompt engineering".

In this post I talk about prompt engineering, share insights from my experience, and describe some commonly used approaches.

Prompt engineering involves a deep understanding of the model's capabilities, idiosyncrasies, and limitations, making it a blend of both science and art. Implementing apps that use prompt engineering can be technically challenging, so it requires coding and deployment skills too.
