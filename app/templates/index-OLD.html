Using a GPT for volatility prediction in risk management

AI now has many more permutations of input/output, i.e., text-to-image (OpenAI's DALLÂ·E), and text-to-video (Google's Imagen). I trained a small text-to-text GPT (Karpathy's NanoGPT) on high-frequency OHLC data and it performed well at volatility prediction. This is a very interesting result indeed that suggests LLMs can be trained and used for risk management.

NanoGPT is a smaller and more lightweight version of the larger GPT models, like ChatGPT and GPT-4. It uses a simple bigram language model and a transformer architecture with multi-head self-attention. I convert numbers to text and train the text-to-text nanoGPT to understand the patterns in the data well enough to generate text in the form of OHLC stock price data that can be switched back to numbers and used for complex numeric computations.

AnalystGPT

AnalystGPT provides responses from GPT4. It is hosted as a web app with a Flask framework at the backend, a modular Blueprint structure, and HTML, CSS, and JavaScript at the frontend. It utilizes the OpenAI Python Library to interact with the GPT-4 model and explores AI's potential to perform in different analytical modalities, including analytical (i.e., business analyst), investigative (i.e., investigator), and financial (i.e., financial analyst) using a variety of prompt engineering methods.

In future posts I will look at the improvements to its performance obtained from zero-shot vs few-shot vs Chain-of-Thought (CoT) vs automatic prompt setups, and various prompt optimization methods.

I will also deploy via GKE/nginix to improve performance, and I will probably re-write the UXUI with React JS to obtain a more intuitive analyst experience.
